Abstract—In the era of Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS), ver-
ifying the legitimacy of an operator requires more than just
digital identity; it demands proof of spatiotemporal presence.
Existing authentication mechanisms, such as cryptography and
biometrics, are fundamentally location-agnostic, creating a “spa-
tiotemporal disconnect” that leaves systems vulnerable to remote
spoofing and replay attacks. To bridge this gap, we propose
StarDial, a physical-layer security primitive that leverages the
Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) not as a position-
ing tool, but as a dynamic spatial cipher. By utilizing the
Geometric Theory of Diffraction (GTD), StarDial analyzes the
specific signal fluctuations (C/N0) induced by hand gestures
cutting across satellite Line-of-Sight (LoS) rays. This interaction
creates a “Triple Binding” of user identity, physical time, and
geographic location. We demonstrate that StarDial effectively
detects advanced threats, including generative SDR spoofing and
remote ghost attacks, by identifying the inevitable “Geometric
Mismatch” between fake digital signals and real physical diffrac-
tion patterns.
I. INTRODUCTION
As Cyber-Physical Systems (CPS) become ubiquitous—
ranging from autonomous drones navigating complex airspace
to robotic dogs inspecting remote infrastructure, and critical
grids maintaining synchronous operations—the boundary be-
tween the digital and physical worlds is dissolving [1], [2],
[3]. These systems do not merely process data; they execute
physical operations.
Consequently, the core paradigm of security is shifting: ver-
ifying operator legitimacy can no longer rely solely on “who
you are” (Identity), but must simultaneously confirm “where
and when you are” (Spatiotemporal Context) [4], [5], [6]. In
physical reality, permissions for high-stakes operations—such
as unlocking a geo-fenced perimeter, opening dam gates, or
landing a drone—are intrinsically bound to specific geographic
locations and time windows.
However, existing security authentication architectures (e.g.,
PKI digital signatures or biometrics) suffer from a fundamental
logical flaw: they are Location-Agnostic. Digital keys are
essentially bitstreams decoupled from physical carriers, and
biometric data can be digitized and stored. This means that
while traditional mechanisms can perfectly verify the “cor-
rectness of the key,” they fail to perceive the “physical scene
of the operation.” This dangerous severance between Digital
Privilege and Physical Reality opens a massive backdoor for
attackers [2], [4], [5]. By leveraging Software-Defined Radios
(SDR) to synthesize fake GPS signals, or using Generative
AI to forge sensor data, attackers can digitally “teleport”
to a target site while physically remaining remote. These

“Remote Ghost Attacks” allow adversaries to bypass geo-

become an urgent imperative for CPS security.
To fill this security vacuum, a mechanism is required

an attacker can physically occupy the target location and
reconstruct the electromagnetic propagation paths of the entire
celestial hemisphere in real time, simulating this macroscopic
geometric feature is physically infeasible.
Building on this insight, we propose StarDial, a novel
physical-layer security primitive. StarDial is not merely a
defense system against GNSS spoofing, but a mechanism

• Binding to Identity: Different sliding paths cover dif-

• Binding to Time: The satellite constellation is in con-

offense and defense. In traditional GNSS spoofing scenarios,
an attacker only needs to generate fake digital signals to
deceive the receiver’s solving logic. In StarDial’s threat model,
the attacker faces a physical “Impossible Triangle”: even
if they use SDR to generate fake GPS coordinate signals,
they cannot physically reconstruct the true electromagnetic
incidence paths distributed across the real sky. When a user’s
hand passes over the antenna, a Geometric Mismatch occurs
between the real physical shadowing (caused by the hand) and
the fake virtual geometry (implied by the spoofed signals).
This contradiction, guaranteed by physical laws, serves as
strong evidence for detecting high-level spoofing attacks.

II. BACKGROUND & MOTIVATION
This section introduces the fundamental physical properties
of GNSS signal propagation and the key metrics of receiver
observables, followed by the definition of attack scenarios,
attacker capabilities, and security assumptions considered in
this work [10], [7], [15].
A. GNSS Primer: Signal Power and Geometric Diversity
Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) broadcast ra-
dio signals from constellations in Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)
[11]. For ground-based receivers, the system provides two key
physical attributes that form the foundation of StarDial.

wavefront arriving at the ground can be treated as a Plane

strength [11], [16]. Under Line-of-Sight (LoS) conditions,
C/N0 typically remains stable. However, when an obstacle
(such as a human hand) exists in the propagation path, the
signal undergoes diffraction and attenuation.
As shown in Fig. 2, according to the Geometric Theory of
Diffraction (GTD), this near-field shadowing is not a simple
binary “on/off” effect. Instead, it induces specific continuous
fluctuations in C/N0, where the fluctuation pattern is strictly
correlated with the distance of the obstacle edge relative to the
LoS ray [13], [14]. This makes C/N0 an ideal physical probe
for sensing minute near-field geometric changes.
2) Dynamic 3D Geometry: At any time instance t, the
visible satellites above the receiver form a discrete spatial
distribution. We model this distribution as a 3D LoS Vector
Field centered at the receiver [7], [12]. This vector field
possesses two significant security properties:
• Spatial Uniqueness: Observers at different geographic
locations p perceive different satellite azimuths and ele-
vations; thus, the geometry is a function of location.
• Temporal Variance: Due to the high-speed motion of
satellites, the vector field evolves continuously over time.
This instantaneous geometric configuration constitutes a
natural, unpredictable source of entropy.
B. Physical Basis and Observable Model
GNSS satellites broadcast L-band signals with wavelengths
of approximately λ ≈19 ∼24 cm. Since the satellite-
receiver distance is much larger than the scale of the local
interaction region, the incident GNSS signals at the ground can
be approximated as plane waves. When a user’s hand enters
the near-field region above the receiver antenna, it behaves not
merely as a simple blocker, but as a finite knife-edge obstacle
that perturbs the Line-of-Sight (LoS) propagation path.
The total electric field at the receiver is therefore the
coherent superposition of the direct LoS component and the
scattered/diffracted components generated by the hand edges.

C. Threat Model
Our goal is to defend against attackers attempting to deceive
the StarDial system to gain unauthorized access. We consider
three primary attack types, with their core characteristics
illustrated in Fig. 3.
1) Generative Spoofing (SDR Attacks): This represents the
most advanced GNSS threat. Attackers use Software-Defined
Radios (SDR) and open-source tools (e.g., GPS-SDR-SIM) to
synthesize fake RF signals complying with standard protocols.
• Goal: The attacker is at Location A but forges the illusion
that the receiver is at Target Location B to bypass geo-
fencing.
• Attack Capability: As shown in Fig. 3(b), the attacker
transmits forged GNSS-like RF signals from a local SDR
source to manipulate the receiver’s perceived position.
• Physical Contradiction: Although forged SDR signals
can manipulate digital GNSS observations, they are typi-
cally emitted from a single source or a limited directional
cluster rather than from the sky-distributed geometry of
real satellites. Consequently, they cannot reproduce the
true multi-directional LoS incidence structure. When the
user performs a gesture above the antenna, the result-
ing perturbation pattern becomes inconsistent with the
claimed satellite geometry, leading to a spatial mismatch,
as illustrated in Fig. 3(b).
2) Replay Attacks: In a standard replay attack, the attacker
typically records only ordinary ambient received signals at a
given moment. However, under a more advanced threat model,
the attacker pre-records the raw RF signals (I/Q data) captured
while a legitimate user is performing a gesture at the target
location, and subsequently replays these signals at a later time.
• Goal: Using expired legitimate signals to bypass authen-
tication.
• Attack Capability: As shown in Fig. 3(c), the attacker
re-injects previously captured RF recordings from an
earlier legitimate session into the receiver chain at a later
time.
• Physical Contradiction: Replay attacks may preserve
authentic diffraction signatures from a past legitimate
interaction, but those signatures remain tied to the his-
torical satellite configuration at the time of recording.
Since the constellation evolves continuously, the replayed
waveform no longer matches the current azimuth, eleva-
tion, and projected contact-point geometry. As a result,
replayed signals fail the current consistency check and
manifest as a temporal mismatch, as shown in Fig. 3(c).
3) Remote Ghost Attacks: Attackers intrude into the field
IoT device via the network, attempting to inject fake sensor
data (e.g., tampering with NMEA sentences or C/N0 readings)
from the OS level, completely bypassing the physical antenna.
• Goal: Bypass the physical sensing path and directly forge
observation data to obtain unauthorized authentication.
• Attack Capability: As shown in Fig. 3(d), the attacker
operates at the software or middleware layer and injects
fabricated navigation or signal-quality data without inter-
acting with the antenna or RF front-end.
• Physical Contradiction: Since the forged observations
do not pass through the real antenna and physical channel,
they lack the analog characteristics of genuine near-field
diffraction, including the continuous structures induced
by Fresnel-zone traversal, phase drift, and cooperative
multi-satellite responses. Therefore, they cannot faithfully
reproduce the physical interaction required by StarDial
and are exposed as a physical mismatch, as shown in
Fig. 3(d).
D. Security Assumptions
To clarify StarDial’s security boundaries, we establish the
following core assumptions.
1) Trust Model:
• Untrusted: We do not trust any digital signal content
received (including navigation messages, timestamps, or
solved coordinates), as these can be fully synthesized by
SDR.
• Trusted: We trust the Geometric Theory of Diffraction
(GTD) and Public Ephemeris Data. We assume electro-
magnetic wave propagation follows Maxwell’s equations,
and the true orbital positions of satellites cannot be
physically moved by the attacker.
2) Core Physical Assumption: Inability to Reconstruct 3D
Wavefronts: This is the cornerstone of StarDial’s security. We
assume the attacker cannot construct a massive hemispherical
antenna array around the target receiver to simulate the true
physical Angle of Arrival (AoA) for every satellite. Such
“Sky-Dome” scale infrastructure is impractical in covert attack
scenarios.
III. SYSTEM DESIGN
This section describes how StarDial transforms raw GNSS
observations into authentication decisions through signal pre-
processing, diffraction feature extraction, gesture-plane geo-
metric inversion, and spatiotemporal consistency authentica-
tion.
A. Signal Preprocessing
Due to the complexity of outdoor environments, raw GNSS
observational data is typically non-uniformly sampled and
noise-corrupted. The objective of the preprocessing module
is to transform raw observations from multiple constellations
into aligned, clean Carrier-to-Noise Ratio (C/N0) waveforms.

2) Baseline-based Cleaning: The C/N0 values reported by
commercial u-blox receivers are typically presented as integers
(e.g., 35–50 dB-Hz). In an unobstructed static environment,
owing to the receiver’s internal gain control mechanism, the
signal generally maintains a highly stable “baseline.” When
a hand gesture approaches or intersects the satellite Line-of-
Sight (LoS) path, the signal not only undergoes severe step-like
degradation but also exhibits distinctive diffraction fluctuations
as the hand enters the satellite’s Fresnel zone. Leveraging this
physical characteristic, StarDial introduces a physics-informed
baseline-based cleaning method. This approach establishes a
physical baseline by statistically analyzing and extracting the
background features of each satellite within the observation
window. Subsequently, the system employs edge-preserving
denoising logic to filter out transient thermal noise and random
spikes that fall below a significance threshold. Coupled with
a sliding window for trend consistency verification, it ensures
the retention of only the continuous fluctuations induced by
gesture dynamics.

servation, the waveform of a given satellite may contain
multiple temporally separated local fluctuations corresponding
to different gesture actions. To capture such action-related
responses from continuous data, StarDial explicitly detects and
characterizes local perturbation segments along the time axis.

D. Spatiotemporal Consistency Authentication
This module determines whether the reconstructed trajec-
tory corresponds to a legitimate user action performed under
genuine physical interaction. StarDial combines template-level
gesture discrimination with physical-layer consistency valida-
tion to make the final authentication decision.
It is important to emphasize that StarDial does not authen-
ticate a user solely based on the similarity of a recovered
gesture shape. Instead, authentication depends on whether the
reconstructed trajectory within the current candidate interval
is consistent with the extracted local perturbation segments
under the instantaneous satellite geometry. Since GNSS con-
stellations are continuously moving, the satellite azimuths, el-
evations, and the corresponding LoS projection contact points
observed by the receiver vary over time. As a result, even if a
user performs the same gesture, the satellites that generate
salient perturbation segments and the associated geometric
constraints on the gesture plane are not identical at different
time instants. This time-evolving satellite distribution binds
the authentication result to both the current location and the
current time, thereby enabling spatiotemporal consistency. The
same temporal geometric dependency also makes it difficult
for historically recorded legitimate signals to be successfully
replayed at a later time.

IV. EVALUATION
A. Experimental Setup
We implemented StarDial on a real GNSS sensing platform
to evaluate near-field gesture sensing and trajectory recon-
struction under practical conditions. The platform consists
of a u-blox F9P multi-band GNSS receiver, an industrial-
grade “mushroom” antenna, and a laptop with an Intel i5
processor and 16 GB of RAM for data logging and back-
end processing. During experiments, the receiver continu-
ously outputs raw multi-constellation observations, including
Carrier-to-Noise Ratio (C/N0), pseudorange, carrier phase,
and satellite geometry. All raw observations were logged at
25 Hz. Subjects performed predefined 2D gestures within
a designated interaction area above the antenna, while the
system synchronously recorded the GNSS observations and
the corresponding ground-truth trajectories.
We evaluated StarDial in three representative outdoor envi-
ronments: an open rooftop, an area between tall buildings,
and a location near trees. The rooftop provides a clean
baseline with high sky visibility and limited static occlusion.
The near-building setting introduces reduced sky visibility,
stronger reflections, and more severe occlusion. The near-trees
setting introduces irregular natural blockage caused by foliage
and branches. Together, these scenarios cover representative
outdoor propagation conditions for assessing sensing and
authentication robustness.
B. Selection of Gesture Plane Height
We first determine the operating height of the gesture
plane. To this end, we scan h from 10 cm to 50 cm and
evaluate both the sparsity of the Geometry Contact Map and
the reconstruction error. As shown in Fig. 6(a), increasing h
reduces the average number of satellites mapped to each grid
cell, indicating a progressively sparser contact map and less
local geometric aliasing.
This benefit, however, comes at the cost of reduced ob-
servational redundancy. Fig. 6(b) shows that the number of
gesture-affected satellites decreases with h, which weakens
the multi-satellite constraints required for stable inversion. As
a result, RMSE exhibits a non-monotonic trend: it is high
at small h due to dense projection overlap, decreases as the
contact map becomes better separated, and rises again when
too few satellites remain perturbed.
Overall, 30 cm provides the best trade-off between geomet-
ric separability and observational redundancy. At this height,
the contact map is sufficiently sparse while the reconstruction
error remains near its minimum. We therefore adopt 30 cm
as the default gesture-plane height, consistent with the setting
used in the system design.
C. Trajectory Reconstruction and Authentication Performance
under Legitimate Access
We next evaluate StarDial under legitimate access, focusing
on trajectory reconstruction quality and authentication per-
formance. Reconstruction quality is measured using MTE,
RMSE, and DTW, while authentication is evaluated through
template matching combined with physical-layer consistency
constraints.
Fig. 7 shows that StarDial reliably preserves the global
shapes of representative 2D gestures. Reconstruction is most
accurate for simple single-stroke trajectories and remains
stable for closed-loop and polyline gestures. The main errors
occur in trajectories with multiple sharp turns, inflection
points, or crossing structures, where local deviations become
more pronounced.

1) Experimental Construction of SDR Generative Spoofing:
For SDR spoofing, the attacker transmits forged GNSS-like RF
signals from a single source or a limited directional cluster. We
construct two representative datasets: Full-occlusion Forgery,
where all visible satellites are forced into a hit state, and
Directional-occlusion Forgery, where only satellites aligned
with a preset azimuth are marked as occluded. Although
such attacks can synthesize protocol-compliant bitstreams,
they cannot reproduce the true multi-directional LoS geometry.
Consequently, their perturbation patterns collapse to either
near-uniform states or directionally concentrated structures,
both of which differ fundamentally from those induced by
a real gesture. Moreover, reproducing Fresnel-like oscilla-
tions would require millisecond-level consistency among hand
motion, intersection geometry, and relative phase, which is
infeasible under single-source transmission.

sider two replay settings. In Gesture-less Data Replay, the
attacker replays ordinary historical GNSS observations without
any gesture-induced perturbation, testing whether a normal
background sequence can bypass authentication. In Gesture-
inclusive Data Replay, the attacker replays a complete his-
torical sequence recorded while a legitimate user performed
a gesture at time told. Although this second case contains
genuine diffraction signatures, those signatures remain tied
to the historical satellite geometry rather than the current
constellation.

V. RELATED WORK
A. GNSS-Based Passive Sensing
The closest system-level neighbor to StarDial is GPSense,
which demonstrates that pervasive GPS/GNSS signals can be
exploited for passive wireless sensing. GPSense reconstructs
sensing-relevant signal variations from raw measurements re-
ported by commodity GNSS receivers, develops GPS-specific
reflection and diffraction models, and leverages multi-satellite
observations for applications such as activity recognition, pas-
sive trajectory tracking, and respiration monitoring [?]. This
line of work is highly relevant because it shows that GNSS
signals can serve as a viable sensing modality beyond position-
ing. However, its goal is fundamentally different from ours:
GPSense is designed for sensing and recognition, whereas
StarDial is designed for authentication. In particular, StarDial
does not merely infer motion from GNSS perturbations; it uses
gesture-induced diffraction as a security primitive to prove
that a claimed interaction is physically consistent with the
instantaneous satellite geometry at a specific time and location.
B. GNSS Anti-Spoofing and Signal Authentication
A large body of prior work has studied GNSS spoofing
detection, signal authentication, and secure localization. Exist-
ing approaches include signal-quality monitoring, navigation-
message authentication, sensor-fusion-based verification, and
geometric consistency checks for protecting positioning in-
tegrity. These methods are effective for determining whether a
GNSS signal or the resulting location solution is trustworthy,
but they mainly focus on the authenticity of navigation signals
and localization outputs. In contrast, StarDial is not merely
a GNSS anti-spoofing mechanism. Instead, it authenticates
whether a user’s physical interaction is jointly consistent with
the current satellite geometry, the receiver’s location, and the
underlying propagation process. Thus, StarDial complements
existing GNSS security techniques by introducing a human-
centered proof of spatiotemporal presence.
C. Physical-Layer Authentication, Location Verification, and
Wireless Sensing
Another related line of work includes physical-layer authen-
tication, RF fingerprinting, location verification, and wireless
sensing systems for gesture or behavior recognition. Physical-
layer authentication typically identifies a transmitter or com-
munication device from channel characteristics or hardware-
specific RF signatures, while location-verification systems
focus on whether a device is at a claimed position. Wireless
sensing systems, in turn, infer motion or behavior from signal
perturbations. Although these directions are closely related,
they usually address only one aspect of the problem: device
identity, location, or motion sensing. StarDial differs from all
three by establishing a triple binding among gesture identity,
physical time, and geographic location. Rather than authen-
ticating a transmitter or only recognizing a motion pattern,
StarDial authenticates the physical consistency of the human–
satellite–receiver interaction under the instantaneous GNSS
constellation.
VI. DISCUSSION & LIMITATIONS
While StarDial demonstrates the feasibility of achieving
spatiotemporal consistency authentication based on GNSS
signals, the system still faces several limitations.


===== PAGE 12 =====
Environmental Dependency: The core mechanism of Star-
Dial is built upon stable GNSS observations and multi-satellite
visibility; therefore, its applicable scenarios are primarily
restricted to outdoor or semi-open environments. In indoor
settings, underground spaces, tunnels, or other scenarios where
GNSS signals are severely attenuated, the receiver struggles to
obtain stable raw observational data. Consequently, the system
cannot reliably perceive the near-field perturbations induced
by hand gestures. Thus, the current solution is better suited as
an authentication mechanism for outdoor CPS scenarios rather
than typical indoor interaction environments.
Sensitivity to Sky Visibility and Multipath Conditions:
Even in outdoor environments, the performance of StarDial
remains susceptible to sky visibility, static occlusions, and
environmental multipath effects. When the experimental site
is surrounded by tall buildings, metallic structures, or complex
reflective surfaces, environmental propagation effects may
weaken or even mask the local diffraction features induced by
the gesture itself. This interference subsequently affects tra-
jectory reconstruction and final authentication results. In other
words, while the current system achieves stable performance in
open-sky scenarios, its robustness in environments with poor
sky visibility or strong multipath interference requires further
validation and improvement.
Reliance on Simplified Interaction Geometry Assumptions:
In the geometric inversion process, the current system adopts
the assumption of a fixed-height gesture plane, approximating
the user interaction as a 2D trajectory within a specific hori-
zontal plane above the antenna. While this design significantly
reduces inversion complexity and enables real-time recovery,
it also limits the system’s adaptability to unconstrained 3D
movements. When a user’s gesture involves significant height
variations, tilted writing, wrist rotations, or fluctuations in
body posture, deviations arise between the actual trajectory
and the model assumptions, thereby affecting reconstruction
accuracy and authentication stability. Consequently, at this
stage, StarDial is more suitable for constrained near-field 2D
interactions and offers limited support for more natural and
free-form 3D interactions.
VII. CONCLUSION
We introduced StarDial, a GNSS-based spatiotemporal
authentication mechanism that turns gesture-induced signal
perturbations into proofs of physical presence. By binding user
interaction to the instantaneous satellite geometry, StarDial
goes beyond conventional location-agnostic authentication and
establishes a triple binding among identity, time, and space.
Through GTD-based modeling, geometric inversion, and
consistency authentication, StarDial shows that legitimate in-
teractions can be distinguished from spoofing, replay, and
ghost injection attacks by their inevitable spatial, temporal,
or physical mismatches. Experimental results on commodity
GNSS hardware demonstrate the feasibility of this design in
real outdoor environments.
Our results indicate that GNSS can serve not only as a
positioning infrastructure, but also as a security substrate for
physically grounded authentication. We hope StarDial will
inspire future research on leveraging large-scale ambient wire-
less infrastructures for security primitives that are inherently
tied to the physical world.
